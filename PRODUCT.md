# Product

## Register

product

## Users

The adult child of an elderly parent (Indonesian family context) — digitally fluent, the paying customer, cannot visit or coach their parent daily in person. They use the app in short check-in bursts (morning coffee, commute, before bed) to see how their parent is doing, not as a full-attention task. Under stress during an alert, they need the critical info immediately, not buried in a dashboard.

## Product Purpose

Lively delivers eldercare through a channel the parent already uses (WhatsApp), while giving the family member visibility and control via this companion app. Four core jobs: set up and switch the WhatsApp companion, monitor the ongoing conversation (read-only), track health progress (30s Chair Stand score, engagement streak, medication adherence), and act on alerts (missed day, missed medication, pain/dizziness mention, emergency). Success = the family member checks in because they *want* to see progress, not because it feels like an audit chore.

## Brand Personality

Warm, reassuring, quietly motivating. Feels like a trusted family health companion, not a hospital chart and not a toy. Gamification (streaks, progress rings, chair-test scores over time) exists to make check-ins something to look forward to — but every gamified element must still read as credible for a health product aimed at an elderly parent's wellbeing.

Reference: pastel, soft-card wellness apps (warm neutrals, rounded surfaces, calm emotional tone) crossed with iOS-native polish and Duolingo-style interactive gamification — never at the expense of trust.

## Anti-references

- Generic Bootstrap/Material default look: flat gray cards, default blue buttons, no real type scale — the "unstyled admin panel" feel.
- Cluttered dashboard: too many stats/cards crammed onto one screen with no breathing room — this is explicitly what the current UI does wrong today.
- Childish/toy-like gamification: cartoon mascots, loud primary colors, confetti overload. Playful, not juvenile — this is a health product for someone's parent.

## Design Principles

1. **Show, don't audit** — progress and streaks are framed as good news to check, not a compliance report on the parent.
2. **One glance, one answer** — every screen answers "how's Eyang doing?" immediately; no digging through nested cards to find the signal.
3. **Escalation clarity over uniformity** — an emergency alert must read as unmistakably more urgent than a missed-day nudge; never let the alert list flatten severity into sameness.
4. **Trust over cuteness** — gamified elements (streaks, rings, badges) support credibility, they never undercut it with cartoonish or gimmicky execution.
5. **Dignity by proxy** — the parent never opens this app, but copy and tone should still read as if written with their dignity in mind, not just their child's convenience.

## Accessibility & Inclusion

Standard mobile accessibility: visible focus states, WCAG AA contrast minimums, Dynamic Type / font-scaling support, VoiceOver/TalkBack labels on icon-only controls. No elder-specific accommodations needed in this app itself since the parent never uses it directly — but alert screens should bias toward higher contrast and larger tap targets given they're often read quickly or under stress.
